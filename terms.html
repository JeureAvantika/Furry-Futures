➢ Adoption Fee:
Most shelters charge an adoption fee to help cover the costs of caring for the animals.
This fee may vary depending on the shelter and the age/health of the dog.

➢ Home Visit:
Some shelters may conduct a home visit to ensure that the environment is suitable for the dog.
They may look for things like a secure fence, adequate space, and a safe living environment.

➢ Vaccinations and Microchipping:
Adopted dogs are typically up to date on vaccinations and may be microchipped for identification purposes.
Some shelters may require adopters to continue with a vaccination schedule or register the microchip in their name.

➢ Behavioural Assessments:
Shelters may perform behavioural assessments on dogs to determine their temperament and suitability for adoption.
They may provide information on any behavioural issues and offer guidance on training and socialization.

➢ Return Policy:
Shelters often have a return policy in case the adoption doesn't work out.
This could involve a trial period during which the dog can be returned if necessary,
or a commitment to take the dog back at any time if the adopter can no longer care for it.

➢ Agreement to Care:
Adopters typically agree to provide proper care for the dog, including regular veterinary check-ups,
appropriate nutrition, exercise, and companionship. They may also agree not to engage in activities
that could harm the dog, such as neglect or abuse.

➢ Legal Responsibilities:
Adopters usually agree to comply with local laws and regulations regarding pet ownership,
including licensing and leash laws.

➢ Ownership Transfer:
Adopters sign paperwork transferring ownership of the dog from the shelter to themselves.
This document may include details about the dog's medical history, any known behavioural issues,
and the terms of the adoption agreement.

➢ Education and Support:
Shelters may provide educational resources and support to help adopters care for their new dog.
This could include information on training, behaviour modification, and health care.
